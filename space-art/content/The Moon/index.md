The Moon, as the closest body to the Earth, has had significance in human culture and society for thousands, if not hundreds of thousands of years. Both Ancient religions as well as many currently established religions have deities that represent the moon. As early as 4500 BCE, Sumerian civilization began to worship a god, Nanna who represented the moon. In the centuries and millennia following, many different religions contributed their own interpretation of a lunar deity to the collective societal consciousness. We as a society are fascinated with the Moon, keeping track of the phases of the moon, eclipses and more. As the planetary object closest to earth, the Moon represents a part of the wider Solar System, the only one that can be easily seen from down on Earth. As humanity has explored the moon with both robotic probes over the years and humans from both the Apollo and Artemis programs. The exploration of the moon by these programs has not just allowed us to see the Moon, but have also allowed us to see the Earth in a new light from our closest neighbor. Even though the Moon is the closest of all the planetary objects to the Earth, being about 250,000 miles away, it is still so far away from the Earth that traveling there takes several days and a significant amount of power and equipment. Looking back at the Earth from the moon, we can see the deep blackness of the vacuum of space- but we also can see just what an incredible gift that this colorful beautiful planet that we call home is.
